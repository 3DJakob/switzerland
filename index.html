COUNTRY & TOWN

Switzerland is a country situated in Central Europe adjacent to France, Germany, Italy, Austria and Liechtenstein. The capital city is Bern even though Zürich is bigger. It has a population of over 8 million people and the official languages are German, French, Italian and Romansh. Having so many official languages strongly effects the country as many people speak different languages depending on the region and situation. Therefore it is mandatory to learn two of the official languages in school as well as English.

The country is divided into 26 cantons with Bern as seat of the federal authorities. Together the cantons make up an area of 41,285 km2 with a max north to south distance of 220km.

I was placed in the town of Rheinfelden just beside the German border defined by the river Rhine and not far from France. Rheinfelden is a small town with 11 596 inhabitants. The town mostly consist of big apartment buildings and tightly placed houses. The area serves mostly as a residential area and it seems as though most people transit from there every morning.

From Rheinfelden there is a 1 hour train ride to Zurich which also stops in places like Baden and Brugg. My daily commute consisted of a buss ride to the train station followed by a 30 minutes train ride to Brugg where I finally switched to a train going to my workplace in Birr. It becomes very apparent that the train infrastructure is very well developed and you can basically go anywhere by train. The trains are always very punctual which is crucial for this type of structure.

WORK

Company

I worked at the company libs Industrielle Berufslehren Schweiz which is a school focused on practical studies for future work applications. Their vision is to help students to be professional learners and to utilise each students full potential.

One thing thats very different from here in Sweden is the fact that the students or apprentices as they are called go in school for 2 days and then work 3 days with their respective profession. That gives the students a unique work experience compared to other schools and gives them a way to ease in to the full work life that will eventually follow.

Working hours

At Libs the students have longer days than back in Sweden. The day starts at 7 am and ends at 4 pm. Beyond that they also have shorter breaks adding up to a total of only 55 minutes. That results in 8 hours and 5 minutes of work per day compared to Swedens measly 6 hours and 20 minutes. This greatly increases the amount of things the students have time to learn and is required to be able to set aside 3 days for work.

Environment

I had my internship at the electrical department of Libs where the students learn about relays, plcs, pneumatics, components, building circuits and so on. Each student gets his/her own workbench with a load out of useful tools like wrenches, screwdrivers and pliers. The students then retrieves the material needed for their project. Everyone works in their own tempo and it was not uncommon to see five people each quietly at their bench working at completely different things.

Hierarchy

The students are guided by mentors who give out and supervise the tasks at hand. They evaluate the students work and verbal presentations of the task is quite common. What's key here is that the mentors tailors a work plan for every specific student after their individual needs. During the whole education programme there are a few selected categories with assignments that every student have to finish. This can however be done in any order. That way student can have way more authority over what they work with at the moment compared to Sweden.

The fact that the teachers have the title mentors is also reflected in the way they work. You learn by doing and the mentors are more like a guide helping you get back on the right track rather than teaching you straight away.

Gender equality

At the workplace in Birr where I was situated there were no girls taking the automation technician class. This however is not due to any gender rules but rather a lack of interest among girls.

This is something that’s changed faster in Sweden. Mechanical subjects and engineering as a work is way more popular among females in Sweden although males have the majority. This is in both countries mostly because of the remains of older times where women weren’t considered capable of working that domain.

Career possibilities

The education plan at Libs stretches across 4 years the 2 first being work at school and then the 2 last an internship at a company like ABB, Siemens, General Electrics and so on. After the 4 years the students are guaranteed a job in their area of expertise.

LEAN

Lean is a way of working with a company’s effectivity by visualising and eliminating waste. During my stay I made some observations both good and bad.

Brakes are very punctual and students honor the schedule. Immediately when the brake starts everyone just drops everything and walks to the breakroom and once it’s over everyone storms out exactly in time.

The students work really intense during work hours and mostly do not slack off or chat with co-workers.

Breaks during the day are very short which increases the time available. The only downside by this is that the students might find it hard to relax during breaks as well as focus during the workshops.

A really long time is spent on cleaning which does not produce any products. Keeping the workplace organised is very important in lean but because the long time available for cleaning people work in a very slow pace. This could be solved by adding schedules for the different areas that needs to be cleaned to make everyone responsible for a specific task.

There are a lot of tasks with very repetitive work which does not teach the student anything more but just takes time. To get the right balance is hard though since people learn in different phases. This problem is partially solved by the fact that the mentors choose which assignment the person in question should do to learn as fast as possible. However many of the assignment in themselves could use some refinement. One of the first tasks I got to complete was making a simple serial and parallel circuit using high gauge wires and crimp terminals. The circuit diagram was filled with unnecessary cable connections to teach you more about crimping but it would have been better to have more assignments stepping up the circuit design and function while teaching you more about crimping.

CROSS CULTURAL ASPECTS

One of the most interesting cultural differences between Sweden and Switzerland quickly turned out to simply be the lack of them.

Switzerland seems to share a lot of the things I always regarded as typical Swedish like avoiding sitting next to another person on the buss or train, keeping distance on the platform while waiting and the level of politeness and lack of words when meeting stranger at places like the workplace.

One big difference however is how accepted smoking is. In Sweden smoking is disregarded by many and for those who smoke they have a responsibility to move away from others. In Switzerland smoking is very common and it was not uncommon to see somebody taking a smoke at the seat next to you on the station causing a need to move.

Punctuality when it comes to schedules is also way more important in Switzerland as stated above with the breaks. In Sweden it’s normal to stop and talk to your friends if you pass them in the hall or maybe sit a minute or two extra in the break room to finish a discussion.
